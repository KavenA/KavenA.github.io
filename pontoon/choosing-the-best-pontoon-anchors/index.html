Choosing the Best Pontoon Anchors

People tend to disregard the anchor as a secondary item. It’s a big mistake. While the selection of an anchor for your pontoon definitely won’t require as much thought as what went into the boat itself, it’s still a highly critical piece of equipment. It’s the one that holds your boat still and that keeps it from crashing into the land after a strong wind.

There’s a lot of different anchor types and a lot of different available weights. You want your’s to be heavy enough but then again, you don’t it to be oversized. An oversized anchor can become a nightmare to recover by hand if things go wrong.

Box anchors

A very popular option for pontoons, the guys over at PontoonForums.com swear by it.

Honestly, box anchors might just be the best overall choice for pontoons, their unique design seem to stick and hold extremely well on soft bottoms. And that’s a thing with anchors; their weight has an impact on firm holding but not as big of an impact as design. The spikes on box anchors seem to grab almost instantly.

Fluke anchors

The fluke or the Danforth style anchor, another popular option for pontoons. It’s really good when the surface is mostly mud it’s got great holding power for it’s weight due to the arms. The negative is that the shape can be unwieldy; you can’t simply store it in a milk crate like the anchors that have an uniform shape.

Mushroom anchors

This one can work in a muddy or in a silt bottom it’s inferior to anchors that are designed to dig. When it’s completely buried in mud, it holds well but you can’t pull it back as easily as a box anchor or a fluke anchor of equivalent weight. You’re basically pulling all the weight of the mud covering it with it.

Anchors with spikes or flukes are better for digging.

River anchors

A modified mushroom anchor with three flukes.

Adequate on a lot of bottoms but just like the mushroom anchor, I’ve heard about people having problems when it’s used on a muddy bottom. The mud sticks to the anchor hardcore and it can become really, really hard to pull back up to the pontoon. Still a major improvement over the mushroom type.

Grapnel anchors

The grappling one is made specifically for bottoms with a lot of rocks, weeds or roots. It’s not made for digging, but it’s really good at resetting itself should the hold break from a strong wind.

Not the most common type of bottom in lakes and rivers.

Should I use an anchor winch?

It depends. For a big pontoon with a heavy anchor, you might want to give your back and your arms a chance and get an electric winch. I know some people absolutely love it.

If you get a winch, you’ll probably want to install it on the bow.
